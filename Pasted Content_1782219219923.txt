Create a website about my business Vantage Clinical Strategy Ltd  Targeting CEO'S AND HRD'S  - Facts — legal position to anchor the corporate message
Your core offer is commercially strong, but the legal wording needs tightening.
Use this framing:
“Employers already have a legal duty to assess and manage work-related stress and mental health risks. Where an employer has five or more employees, significant risk-assessment findings must be recorded. The Employment Rights Act reforms increase the employment-litigation exposure from 2026–27, including changes to tribunal time limits and, from January 2027, reduced unfair-dismissal qualifying periods and uncapped compensatory awards.”
Why this matters:
•	HSE states that employers have a legal duty to protect workers from work-related stress by doing a risk assessment and acting on it. 
•	Employers with five or more people must record significant risk-assessment findings. 
•	The UK government’s April 2026 implementation update says employment tribunal time-limit changes take effect no earlier than October 2026, and from 1 January 2027 unfair-dismissal qualifying periods reduce to six months and compensatory awards are uncapped. 
•	There is no limit in discrimination cases at employment tribunal. 
•	Poor mental health costs UK employers about £51bn per year, with presenteeism the largest component at around £24bn. 
Critical correction
Do not say:
“Day-one rights mean any employee can sue from day one for psychosocial risk.”
That is too loose and could undermine credibility with HR directors, employment lawyers, insurers and boards.
Say instead:
“The direction of travel is clear: lower barriers to claims, higher potential awards, and greater scrutiny of whether employers can evidence that they identified, assessed and controlled psychosocial risks.”
That is more defensible.

Board-level risk map
1.	Legal and tribunal exposure
Risk managed
An employee alleges that workplace conditions caused or worsened stress, anxiety, depression, burnout, trauma, harassment impact, disability-related deterioration, or psychiatric injury.
Corporate consequence
•	Employment tribunal claim.

•	Whistleblowing or grievance escalation.

What the audit gives them
•	Evidence that risks were considered before harm escalated. 
•	A documented trail of reasonable steps. 
•	Clear manager guidance. 
•	Reduced “we did nothing” vulnerability. 
•	Better defence if an employee later says concerns were ignored. 
Corporate language
“This is not a wellbeing initiative. It is a legal defensibility exercise.”

2.	Director and senior-leadership risk
Risk managed
Senior leaders assume mental health risk is an HR issue, when in practice it may sit across health and safety, employment law, culture, operational design and governance.
Corporate consequence

•	HR blamed for operational failures it did not control. 
•	Managers acting inconsistently. 
•	Weak evidence that senior leadership took reasonable steps.

•	Evidence that senior management understood foreseeable psychosocial risks.

Recommended corporate pitch structure
1.	Open with the risk
“Psychosocial risk is now a board-level employment, health-and-safety and financial exposure. The issue is not whether an organisation has a wellbeing policy. The issue is whether it can prove it identified, assessed and controlled foreseeable harm.”
2.	State the commercial problem
“Most organisations have policies. Fewer have evidence that managers know how to apply them, that risks are assessed consistently, and that warning signs are acted on before they become absence, grievance, attrition or litigation.”
3.	Define the service
“We conduct a forensic psychosocial risk audit to identify gaps in legal compliance, management practice, policy implementation and evidence quality. We then upskill senior leaders and managers to reduce exposure and create a defensible record of action.”
4.	Make the board care
“The cost of getting this wrong is not limited to sickness absence. It includes presenteeism, turnover, replacement cost, tribunal exposure, legal fees, reputational damage and uncapped compensation risk in relevant claims.”
5.	Close with the practical outcome
“The output is a clear risk register, prioritised action plan, leadership training, evidence pack and review cycle — so the organisation can show what it knew, what it did, when it acted, and how it monitored improvement.”

What corporate buyers will challenge
1.	“Is this really a legal requirement?”
Best answer
“The legal duty to assess and manage work-related stress already exists under health and safety law. Employment-law reforms increase the practical exposure because claims may become easier to bring and more expensive to lose. This service is designed to help you evidence reasonable steps.”
2.	“Don’t we already have policies?”
Best answer
“Policies are only useful if they are implemented, understood by managers, reviewed, and evidenced. A tribunal or claimant lawyer will not only ask whether a policy existed. They will ask what happened in practice.”
3.	“Isn’t this just wellbeing?”
Best answer
“No. Wellbeing is part of it, but this is a risk-management and legal-defensibility exercise. It connects health and safety, employment law, leadership behaviour, absence, performance, turnover and claims exposure.”
4.	“Can you guarantee we won’t be sued?”
Best answer
“No. No credible adviser can guarantee that. What we can do is reduce foreseeable risk, improve management practice, and strengthen the evidence that the organisation acted reasonably.”
5.	“Why you?”
Best answer
“My background is in complex health and social care, where unmanaged psychosocial risk has real human, operational and regulatory consequences. I have led services through major quality improvement, seen the impact of poor leadership on mental health, and understand how robust systems, documentation and leadership behaviour protect both people and organisations.”

Strongest one-page corporate message
Psychosocial Risk Audit
The risk
Employers are exposed when workplace stress, poor leadership, bullying, unmanaged workload, trauma exposure, role ambiguity or poor communication are not assessed and controlled.
The cost
Failure can lead to:
•	Tribunal and legal claims. 
•	Uncapped compensation in relevant discrimination claims and, from January 2027, uncapped compensatory awards for unfair dismissal. 
•	Absence and presenteeism. 
•	Staff turnover and re-recruitment cost. 
•	Reputational harm. 
•	Productivity loss. 
•	Leadership and governance scrutiny. 
The problem
Most organisations have policies. Many cannot prove those policies are working in practice.
The solution
A forensic audit that tests whether the organisation can evidence that it:
•	Identified psychosocial risks. 
•	Assessed who may be harmed and how. 
•	Took reasonable steps to reduce risk. 
•	Trained managers. 
•	Supported affected employees. 
•	Recorded decisions. 
•	Reviewed controls. 
•	Escalated concerns appropriately. 
The outcome
A legally defensible, operationally practical risk-control system that protects employees, leaders and the organisation.

Recommendations
1.	Reframe from “mental health compliance” to “psychosocial risk defensibility”
This makes the offer more serious, more board-relevant and less likely to be dismissed as wellbeing spend.
2.	Remove or soften overclaims
Avoid saying all employees have day-one unfair-dismissal rights. The stronger position is that employment-law reforms increase exposure and lower barriers over 2026–27.
3.	Package the service around evidence
The corporate buyer needs to know what they will receive:
•	Audit report. 
•	Risk register. 
•	Defensibility score. 
•	Action plan. 
•	Manager training. 
•	Evidence tracker. 
•	Board summary. 
4.	Use a legal partner if possible
Because this touches employment law, your safest commercial model is:
“Clinical and operational psychosocial risk audit, delivered with employment-law review where required.”
That protects credibility and reduces professional-liability risk.
5.	Make the ROI explicit
Use three cost buckets:
•	Claims cost: tribunal, settlement, legal fees, compensation. 
•	Workforce cost: absence, presenteeism, attrition, recruitment. 
•	Reputation cost: employee trust, candidate attraction, stakeholder confidence.

Decision rule for your offer
Proceed with this positioning if…
•	You can produce a clear audit methodology. 
•	You have a solicitor or HR/employment-law partner for legal review. 
•	You can evidence your leadership and clinical credibility. 
•	You can show before/after outputs. 
Pause if…
•	You cannot yet define exactly what the audit tests. 
•	You are relying heavily on “new legislation” without precise wording. 
•	You do not have professional indemnity cover for this type of work. 
Walk away from claims such as…
•	“We guarantee legal compliance.” 
•	“We prevent tribunal claims.” 
•	“Employees cannot sue if you follow our risk assessment.” 
•	“This applies as a day-one right to every psychosocial risk claim.” 
Use:
“We reduce exposure, improve compliance evidence, and strengthen defensibility.”

5.	Make the ROI explicit
Use three cost buckets:
•	Claims cost: tribunal, settlement, legal fees, compensation. 
•	Workforce cost: absence, presenteeism, attrition, recruitment. 
•	Reputation cost: employee trust, candidate attraction, stakeholder confidence.

Decision rule for your offer
Proceed with this positioning if…
•	You can produce a clear audit methodology. 
•	You have a solicitor or HR/employment-law partner for legal review. 
•	You can evidence your leadership and clinical credibility. 
•	You can show before/after outputs. 
Pause if…
•	You cannot yet define exactly what the audit tests. 
•	You are relying heavily on “new legislation” without precise wording. 
•	You do not have professional indemnity cover for this type of work. 
Walk away from claims such as…
•	“We guarantee legal compliance.” 
•	“We prevent tribunal claims.” 
•	“Employees cannot sue if you follow our risk assessment.” 
•	“This applies as a day-one right to every psychosocial risk claim.” 
Use:
“We reduce exposure, improve compliance evidence, and strengthen defensibility.”
